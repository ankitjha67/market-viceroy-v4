# Known failure modes — dividend_yield

## 1. Value trap (high yield = distressed)

Stocks with very high dividend yields are often distressed
companies whose prices have fallen sharply. The high yield signals
financial trouble rather than genuine carry, and these "value
traps" frequently cut dividends and decline further.

## 2. Dividend policy changes

Companies can cut, suspend, or eliminate dividends without warning.
A strategy relying on historical dividend yields can be whipsawed
when a high-yield stock eliminates its dividend (e.g., banks in
2008, energy companies in 2020).

## 3. Tax regime sensitivity

Dividend yield strategies are sensitive to tax policy changes.
Shifts in dividend tax rates (e.g., the 2003 and 2013 US tax
changes) can alter the cross-sectional relationship between
dividend yield and expected returns.

## 4. Proxy conflates carry with low-vol

The Phase 1 proxy (trailing return / trailing volatility)
conflates dividend yield carry with the low-volatility anomaly.
High proxy scores may reflect low volatility rather than high
carry, leading to unintended factor exposure.
